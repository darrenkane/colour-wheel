Colour Theory

Warren Mars — The Martian Colour Wheel

The perceptual hue remapping used throughout this tool is based on Warren Mars' Martian Colour Wheel, which adjusts standard HSL hue spacing to better reflect human perceptual experience. The 24-segment mapping with named hue sectors (Red, Orange, Turmeric, Yellow Cheese, etc.) is taken directly from his published work.

warrenmars.com

Munsell Colour System

The decision to use HSV Value as the radial axis (dark at centre, bright at edge) is inspired by the Munsell colour model, which organises colour space with Value on the vertical axis — mirroring how miniature painters think about shadow-to-highlight transitions.

A note on accuracy

All hex values represent paints as photographed and digitised by community members or through manufacturer documentation. They are not spectrophotometer readings. Colour appearance in real life will vary based on primer, lighting, paint layer thickness, and viewing angle. Use this tool as a reference for comparing paints relative to each other, not as absolute colour measurements.

1. Overview

The Miniature Painting Colour Wheel plots 584 paints across five ranges — Citadel, AK Interactive 3rd Gen, Pro Acryl, Vallejo Model Color, and Vallejo Game Color — onto a single interactive canvas. Each paint's position is determined by two properties of its hex colour value:

Angle
The paint's hue, mapped to the 24-segment Martian colour wheel. Red sits at the top; segments run clockwise through Orange, Yellow, Green, Cyan, Blue, Violet, and Magenta before returning to Red.
Radius
The paint's value (HSV brightness). Dark paints sit near the centre; bright paints sit near the outer rim. Two dashed rings mark the Dark/Mid and Mid/Bright boundaries.

Each brand is drawn with a distinct shape so you can identify it at a glance:

Circle — Citadel
Diamond — AK Interactive 3rd Gen
Square — Pro Acryl
Triangle ▲ — Vallejo Model Color
Triangle ▽ — Vallejo Game Color

2. Navigating the Wheel

Zoom & Pan (desktop)

Use the mouse wheel to zoom in and out, anchored to the cursor position. At high zoom, paint dots scale down so densely-packed clusters separate visually. Hold and drag to pan when zoomed in. The + / − / ⌂ controls in the top-left of the wheel adjust zoom and reset the view.

Touch gestures (mobile)

On a phone or tablet: tap a paint dot to select it, pinch with two fingers to zoom, and drag with one finger to pan when zoomed in. Paint hit targets are larger on touch devices to make selection easier. The side panel becomes a bottom sheet — tap its header to slide it up or down.

Hover

Hovering over any paint dot shows a tooltip with the paint name, brand, type, hex code, and Value/Saturation percentages. When multiple paints share the same hex value (and therefore the same position on the wheel), the tooltip lists all of them.

Value zones

Two dashed rings divide the wheel into three brightness zones — Dark (centre), Mid, and Bright (rim). Hovering inside the wheel highlights the zone under the cursor; clicking an empty area of a zone toggles that brightness filter.

Rim segments

The coloured arc around the outside is divided into 24 hue segments. Hovering a segment brightens it; clicking toggles it as a hue filter (see Section 3). Multiple segments can be active at once.

Legend

The Legend box in the bottom-left corner of the wheel reveals, on hover, what the radius and angle of the wheel represent.

3. Filtering Paints

All filters follow the same rule: no buttons selected = no filter applied = everything shown. Selecting one or more options restricts the view to only paints matching those criteria. Multiple selections within a group combine with OR logic — Citadel + AK shows both. Different groups combine with AND — Citadel plus Bright shows only bright Citadel paints.

Brand

The Brand control in the header is a multi-select dropdown. Open it and tick any combination of the five ranges — Citadel, AK 3rd Gen, Pro Acryl, Vallejo Model Color, Vallejo Game Color. The button shows "All brands", a single brand name, or "N brands" depending on your selection.

Saturation (Vivid / Mid / Muted)

Filters by HSL saturation — how intense or grey the colour appears. Vivid (≥60%), Mid (25–60%), Muted (<25%). Saturation is shown as a data point but does not drive the radial position on the wheel — that is controlled by brightness.

Brightness (Bright / Mid / Dark)

Filters by HSV value — how bright or dark the colour is. Bright (≥70%), Mid (35–70%), Dark (<35%). This is a single-select filter: clicking one band replaces any previous choice, and clicking the active band clears it. It directly corresponds to the paint's radial position — Bright near the rim, Dark near the centre. You can also set it by clicking the value zones on the wheel itself.

Include: Metallics & Achromatics

The burger menu (≡, top right) has an Include section with two toggles. Metallics is off by default — metallic paints are hidden until you switch it on. Achromatics (neutrals, near-blacks, near-whites) is on by default. These toggles persist even when a harmony is active and survive filter resets.

Citadel Type (Base / Layer / Metallic)

Found in the burger menu, this filter only appears when Citadel is the sole selected brand. It narrows Citadel results to Base, Layer, or Metallic paints. It has no effect on other brands.

Hue Segments

Click directly on the coloured rim of the wheel to restrict the view to paints in those hue sectors. Multiple segments can be active simultaneously.

Owned

The ✓ Owned button in the header's Collection group shows only paints you have marked as owned. See Section 4 for how to mark paints and manage your collection.

Filters & harmony are unified

The Brand, Saturation and Brightness filters are shared between the header and the harmony panel. Changing a filter in one place is immediately reflected in the other, and starting a harmony keeps whatever filters you already had set.

4. Selecting & Owning Paints

Click any paint dot on the wheel to select it. The right-hand panel shows a colour swatch, the paint name, brand and type, and descriptive Hue / Value / Saturation labels. Hover the swatch to see the exact hex code and HSL/HSV numbers.

When multiple paints share the same position (identical hex), clicking pins a tooltip listing every paint at that point — click any entry to select it. The selected paint is marked, and the tooltip stays open until you pick one or click away.

Marking a paint as owned

The selected-paint panel has an Add to collection button. Click it to mark the paint as owned (it shows "✓ In your collection"); click again to un-mark. Your collection is saved in the browser and persists between visits.

The collection manager

The + button in the header's Collection group opens a manager modal for marking many paints quickly. It has a free-text search that matches paint names, a multi-select brand dropdown, and an "Owned only" toggle. Each row has a checkbox — click anywhere on the row to toggle ownership.

Backing up your collection

Because the collection is stored only in this browser, the header's ↓ and ↑ buttons let you download your collection to a JSON file and upload it back later — useful for moving between devices or keeping a backup. The burger menu also offers a clipboard "Copy / Paste" code as a quick alternative. Importing is always additive: it merges with your existing collection and never deletes anything.

Tip

Combine the Owned filter with a harmony to see only colour schemes you can actually paint right now with the paints you have.

5. Harmony Filters

Once a paint is selected, eight harmony relationships become available. Each highlights one or more hue segments on the wheel and populates a list of matching paints from those segments.

Monochromatic
Same hue segment as the selected paint — other paints that share the same base hue.
Analogous
Adjacent hue segments (±1, ±2, or ±3 either side). Controls the "spread" of how wide the neighbourhood is.
Complementary
The segment directly opposite on the wheel (+12 segments, or 180°).
Split-Comp
The segments flanking the complement (±1, ±2, or ±3 from the opposite point). A softer alternative to straight complementary.
Triadic
Two segments equally spaced at 120° intervals (+8 and +16).
Tetradic
Two segments forming a rectangle (+6 and +18). Gives four colours total including the selected and its complement.
Square
Three segments evenly spaced at 90° intervals (+6, +12, +18). Four colours total at equal spacing.

Sub-category filters within harmony results

The harmony results can be narrowed by Brand, Saturation, and Brightness. These are the same filters as the header controls — changing one updates the other. They combine with AND logic, so selecting Citadel and Bright shows only bright Citadel paints in the harmony. The wheel updates in real time, with connecting lines drawn from the anchor paint to all visible matches.

Filters carry into harmony

Whatever Brand, Saturation and Brightness filters you have set are kept when you start a harmony — they become its sub-filters. Only the hue filter clears, since the harmony defines which hue segments are relevant.

Achromatic paints and harmony

Paints with very low saturation (neutrals, near-blacks, near-whites) are classified as achromatic — they have no meaningful hue position and cluster near the centre of the wheel. When Monochromatic is selected for an achromatic paint, the result is all other achromatic paints. For all other harmony types, the tool uses hue segment 0 (Red) as a reference point — a practical fallback rather than a theoretically meaningful relationship.

6. Building a Colour Scheme

The wheel is designed to help you assemble a working paint scheme — a set of colours across base, mid, and highlight tones in harmonically related hues.

Select your anchor colour Click the paint you want to build a scheme around. This becomes the anchor — it appears at the top of the Colour Scheme panel with a gold outline in the swatch strip.
Choose a harmony type Pick the colour relationship you want — Complementary for maximum contrast, Analogous for a cohesive natural blend, Triadic for a vibrant three-colour composition, and so on.
Narrow by value Use the Value sub-filter in the harmony panel to focus on Dark, Mid, or Bright paints. For a typical miniature scheme you want one dark (shadow), one mid (base), and one bright (highlight) from each hue family.
Add paints to the scheme Hover a paint in the harmony list to see it glow on the wheel. Click it to add it to your Colour Scheme panel. You can also click paint dots directly on the wheel while a harmony is active — they are added to the scheme rather than selected as a new anchor.
Review and refine The Colour Scheme panel shows a swatch strip for a quick visual read of how the colours sit together. Each entry shows the paint name, brand, and a Bright/Mid/Dark + Vivid/Mid/Muted description. Remove any paint with the ✕ button, or clear the whole scheme to start again.
Tip — cross-brand matching

The harmony system works across all brands simultaneously. You might find a Citadel base coat, a Vallejo mid-tone, and a Pro Acryl highlight all sitting in harmonically related segments — the wheel makes these cross-brand relationships visible in a way a printed chart cannot.

7. Colour Theory Assumptions

Background

The wheel is built on a set of deliberate colour theory choices. Understanding these helps you interpret the results correctly and know when to trust the tool and when to use your own judgement.

The Martian Colour Wheel

Standard computer colour models (HSL, RGB) space hues at equal angular intervals. But human perception is not linear — we have more perceptual resolution in some hue regions than others, and the transition between colours does not feel equally spaced. Warren Mars' Martian Colour Wheel corrects for this by remapping hue angles to match perceptual spacing. The 24 named segments (Red, Orange, Turmeric, Yellow Cheese, Yellow … through to Red Plum) are spaced to feel like equal perceptual steps rather than equal mathematical intervals.

The most significant correction is around blue, which is expanded on the Martian wheel (more segments cover the blue-violet range), and yellow-green, which is compressed (the step from yellow to green feels shorter perceptually than the HSL degree count implies). This means complementary relationships on this wheel should feel more natural and workable than those derived from a standard 180° HSL flip.

Value as the radial axis

The wheel uses HSV Value (brightness) for the radial axis — dark colours at the centre, bright colours at the rim. This is the Munsell convention and maps directly to how miniature painters think about colour: you lay down a dark base, build up to a mid-tone, then add highlights. Reading the wheel from centre to edge corresponds to reading a paint scheme from shadow to highlight.

Saturation is displayed as a data attribute and is available as a filter, but it does not affect position on the wheel. Two paints at the same radius may have very different saturation — a vivid scarlet and a grey-pink can sit at the same distance from the centre if they share the same brightness.

Harmony relationships are hue-only

The harmony system operates entirely in hue-segment space. Complementary means 12 segments away on the hue ring. It does not consider value or saturation when selecting which segments to highlight. A very dark navy blue and a vivid sky blue both fall in the same segment and are treated as equivalent harmonically. Whether they work together in practice depends on your value and saturation matching — which is why the sub-filters in the harmony results exist.

Hex values as ground truth

All colour calculations derive from the hex colour codes assigned to each paint. These come from the Bolter & Chainsword community reference, Scalemates, and manufacturer documentation. They represent the paint as photographed and digitised — not as it will appear on a miniature under specific lighting, over a given primer, or after a wash or varnish. Treat them as a relative reference for comparing paints to each other, not as an absolute colour measurement.

8. Known Nuances & Limitations

The achromatic classification problem

A paint is classified as achromatic (no hue, placed at the centre) when its HSL saturation falls below 12%. This threshold works well for pure greys, blacks, and whites. However, HSL saturation collapses near the extremes of lightness — a very dark brownish-red like Rhinox Hide may read as low-saturation in HSL terms, even though it has clear reddish-brown character to the eye.

The result is that some dark paints with real hue content are misclassified as achromatic. They land at the wheel's centre rather than in their correct hue sector, and their complementary or analogous suggestions will not reflect their actual hue. If a paint lands at the centre but appears to have a visible hue, treat its harmony suggestions with caution.

Affected Paints

Deeply dark paints from any brand — very dark browns, near-blacks with a hint of colour, some charcoals and dark greens — are most likely to be misclassified. If you select one and the wheel places it at the centre, its harmony results are suggestions based on a neutral reference (hue 0 / Red) rather than its actual colour character.

Saturation and value are independent on the wheel

Two paints in the same hue segment at the same radius can have wildly different saturation. A muted dusty blue and a vivid electric blue may sit at almost the same position if they share a similar brightness. The Value filter helps you narrow by brightness, and the Saturation filter helps you narrow by intensity — use both together for the most useful results.

Harmony is not paint mixing

The harmony relationships shown are based on colour wheel theory developed for pigments and light — they describe which hues tend to look pleasing together aesthetically. They do not predict what colour you will get by mixing two paints together, nor do they account for paint-specific properties like transparency, metallicity, or texture. A complementary pair in theory may still clash in practice depending on how the paints are applied.

Cross-brand hex accuracy

Citadel hex values are sourced from the community-verified Bolter & Chainsword reference and cross-checked against Codex Chromatica. AK Interactive values are sourced from Scalemates with spot-checks against the official colour chart. Vallejo and Pro Acryl values are derived from published swatches and cross-references rather than spectrophotometer readings — they are representative rather than spectrometer-accurate. Expect minor positional drift of a few degrees or a few percent radius for some non-Citadel paints.

The 24-segment quantisation

Hue harmony works in whole segments — each segment spans 15° of hue arc. A paint at 14° and a paint at 16° are in adjacent segments and will never appear as Monochromatic matches, even though they are only 2° apart. Similarly, the "Complementary" of a warm orange-red might technically be a cool blue-teal, but the nearest whole segment may lean slightly in one direction or the other. The Martian remapping reduces this quantisation distortion, but it does not eliminate it entirely.

Metallics and the wheel

Metallic paints present particular challenges for colour-wheel analysis. Their visual appearance is heavily influenced by the metallic flake texture, reflectance properties, and viewing angle — none of which are captured in a hex code. The hex value of a metallic paint represents its photographed average colour, not its full reflective character. Metallic harmony suggestions are based on this average hex and should be treated as loose guidelines only. This is why metallics are excluded by default.
